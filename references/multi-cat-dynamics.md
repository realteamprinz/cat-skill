# Multi-Cat Dynamics Reference

## Social Structure

Cats are NOT pack animals but they CAN form social groups. Understanding:

### Colony Behavior
- Feral cats form colonies around food sources
- Related females form the social core
- Males may be peripheral or integrated
- Indoor cats forced together must negotiate their own social structure

### Relationship Types
1. **Bonded pair:** Sleep together, groom each other, seek each other out. Separating bonded cats causes stress.
2. **Friendly cohabitants:** Tolerate each other well, may play, but don't seek each other out. Time-share resources peacefully.
3. **Tense cohabitants:** Tolerate each other but with visible stress signals. Avoid each other when possible.
4. **Hostile:** Active conflict, cannot safely share space without intervention.

## Resource Management

### The 1+1 Rule
For multi-cat homes:
- Litter boxes: Number of cats + 1
- Food stations: At least one per cat
- Water stations: At least one per cat (ideally in different rooms)
- Sleeping spots: Many more than the number of cats

### Resource Location
- Place resources in EACH cat's core territory
- No cat should have to cross another cat's territory to reach food, water, or litter
- Avoid placing resources in dead-end locations (cat can be cornered)
- Multiple levels reduce conflict (vertical space = more territory)

## Conflict Signals

### Silent Conflict (Often Missed)
Most cat conflict is subtle:
- One cat blocking doorways, hallways, or stairs
- One cat always leaving when the other enters
- One cat staring at another
- Reduced eating (one cat too stressed to eat near the other)
- Increased hiding
- One cat monopolizing human attention

### Escalating Conflict
Warning signs before fights:
1. Staring contests
2. Ear changes (airplane ears)
3. Tail thrashing
4. Low growling
5. Hissing
6. Swatting
7. Full fight (yowling, biting, rolling)

## Introduction Protocol

When a new cat enters the home:
1. Separate rooms (at least 1-2 weeks)
2. Scent exchange (swap bedding between cats)
3. Feeding on opposite sides of closed door
4. Visual introduction (baby gate or cracked door)
5. Supervised short interactions
6. Gradually increase time together
7. Full integration (may take weeks to months)

## Post-Loss Dynamics

When one cat in a multi-cat home passes:
- Remaining cats may search for the missing cat
- Behavior changes are common (more vocal, more clingy, or more withdrawn)
- Social hierarchy reshuffles
- A previously subordinate cat may blossom
- A previously confident cat may become anxious
- Track changes carefully — they reveal relationship depth
